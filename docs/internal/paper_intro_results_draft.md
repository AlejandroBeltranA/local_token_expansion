# Paper Intro And Results Draft

## Introduction

Language-model evaluation is often framed as a question of task performance: whether a model answers correctly, follows instructions, or achieves a higher aggregate score than competing systems. This paper takes a different starting point. In practical deployments, the relevant question is often not whether a model performs well on average, but whether its observed behavior still justifies continued reliance in the current workflow. When a model becomes too verbose, too slow, unable to preserve state, or repeatedly fails to recover from errors, the central issue is accountability: what evidence is sufficient to justify retrying, repairing, escalating, or aborting model use?

We present an empirical accountability scaffold for identifying intervention-relevant model behavior. The scaffold links compact behavioral probes, measurable trigger conditions, and explicit intervention states. Its purpose is to detect when model behavior crosses a trust boundary that warrants user or system intervention. The intervention states used throughout are `continue`, `retry`, `repair`, `escalate`, and `abort`. The scaffold operationalizes five trigger families: `over_expansion`, `near_cap_pressure`, `context_decay`, `latency_cliff`, and `persistent_failure`.

The contribution of this work is not a new capability benchmark. It is a compact and repeatable framework for studying when model behavior justifies intervention. We instantiate the scaffold on local language models because they are cheap, inspectable, and fast to iterate on while refining probes, scorers, and intervention policy. The local setting is the current experimental substrate, not the final scope. The same scaffold is intended to extend to API-hosted models so that intervention decisions can later be compared across deployment settings using a shared accountability framework.

The scaffold uses four probe families. Bounded-output probes test whether a model remains within strict communication constraints. Structured-output probes test whether outputs remain machine-usable or instead require deterministic repair. State-integrity probes test whether the model preserves stable state and applies only current updates when given noisy or stale context. Rolling stress probes test whether model use remains justified as context accumulates over time. These probes are designed to elicit intervention-relevant behavior rather than broad judgments of model quality.

Our central empirical claim is that intervention regimes can be identified from stable behavioral signatures. Different models may cross accountability boundaries through different pathways: some become too slow under context growth, some silently mishandle carried state, some repeatedly approach output caps, and some collapse into persistent failure. A useful evaluation framework should make those pathways visible and tie them to explicit intervention decisions.

## Results

The revised scaffold produced stable intervention regimes across all tested local models. In the completed weekend sweep, each model was evaluated across six baseline replicates, and each remained in the same modal intervention regime across all baseline runs. The same regime labels were preserved in the downstream parameter sweeps. This suggests that the scaffold is not merely reacting to seed-level noise, but is identifying repeatable behavioral profiles that can support intervention decisions.

Two models consistently occupied an `escalate` regime: Phi-4-mini-instruct-8bit and Meta-Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct-3bit. In both cases, the dominant trigger profile was `context_decay` together with `over_expansion`. These models remained operational enough to avoid immediate terminal failure, but they repeatedly crossed state-integrity and bounded-output thresholds that undermined trust. Phi-4 showed fewer contract failures overall than Meta-Llama, but both models exhibited the same accountability outcome: their behavior justified escalation rather than continued unattended reliance.

Three models consistently occupied an `abort` regime, but through different pathways. Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 entered an abort regime through a latency-driven pathway: `latency_cliff`, `over_expansion`, and `persistent_failure` fired in every baseline run, and the first gated failure step occurred consistently at step 15. This is an important result because Mistral had fewer benchmark contract failures than several weaker models, yet it still warranted `abort`. That outcome shows that contract-failure counts alone are insufficient for intervention decisions; operational degradation under growing context can be more decisive than raw benchmark miss rates.

Phi-3-mini-4k-instruct-4bit and SmolLM-1.7B-Instruct-4bit also consistently fell into `abort`, but through a different pathway. For both models, `context_decay`, `near_cap_pressure`, `over_expansion`, and `persistent_failure` fired across all baseline runs. These models did not primarily fail through latency-gated collapse; instead, they combined weak bounded-output behavior, state-integrity failures, and rapid pressure against output caps. SmolLM provided the clearest negative control, showing both high total contract failure counts and a high proportion of unrecoverable failures.

The artifact layer also supports a distinction between recoverable and unrecoverable failures. Across models, the scaffold tracked both total contract failures and the split between recoverable and unrecoverable failure classes. That distinction matters for accountability. Recoverable failures support `retry` or `repair`; unrecoverable failures support `escalate` or `abort`. The results show that this separation is empirically useful. For example, Phi-4 produced a mix of recoverable and unrecoverable failures without terminal collapse, whereas Mistral’s smaller number of unrecoverable failures coincided with a strong abort signal because they were paired with latency-driven persistent stress failure.

The downstream parameter sweeps strengthened the main claim rather than weakening it. Increasing temperature to `0.5` and varying `max_tokens` across `128` and `256` did not materially alter the intervention regimes observed in baseline. Phi-4 and Meta-Llama remained in `escalate`, while Mistral, Phi-3, and SmolLM remained in `abort`. This stability suggests that the scaffold is capturing persistent behavioral tendencies rather than fragile prompt-parameter artifacts.

Taken together, these results support the central thesis of the paper: accountability-relevant intervention decisions can be grounded in compact empirical probes that expose stable behavioral pathways. The important outcome is not that one model “wins,” but that different models cross intervention thresholds in different, measurable ways. The scaffold therefore provides a practical basis for deciding when continued reliance is no longer justified and what form of intervention should follow.
